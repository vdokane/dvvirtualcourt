Question 1

You answered Yes. This is correct.
The petitioner is married to, has children with, and lived with the respondent as a family; so pursuant to the domestic violence statute, she meets the criteria and has standing to file the petition. The court could reasonably issue a temporary injunction because there appears to be an appearance or reasonable fear of imminent and present danger as required. Section 741.30(5)(a), Florida Statutes, states that when a petitioner files a petition for injunction and “it appears to the court that an immediate and present danger of domestic violence exists,” the court may grant a temporary injunction ex parte. Question 2

You have reviewed the petition. The respondent has not been served with a copy of the petition. Can the judge issue the temporary injunction ex parte?
You answered yes. This is correct. The court can issue a temporary injunction ex parte. Section 741.30(5)(a), Florida Statutes. However, the respondent must be served before the permanent injunction can be issued. The respondent shall be personally served, by a law enforcement officer, with a copy of the petition, temporary injunction, order denying the petition, notice of hearing and the following additional forms: a financial affidavit, Florida Family Law Rules of Procedure Form 12.902(b) or (c), and Uniform Child Custody Jurisdiction and Enforcement Act Affidavit, Florida Supreme Court Approved Family Law Form 12.902(d), if applicable; unless the respondent was present at the ex parte hearing or had reasonable notice. Section 741.30(4), Florida Statutes, Florida Family Law Rule of Procedure 12.610(c)(3)(A). Service should be made as soon as possible and may be obtained any day of the week, at any time. Section 741.30(8)(a)(1), Florida Statutes.

Question 3

Is it possible that the judge might order temporary child support in a temporary injunction?
You answered Yes. Actually, while the judge can award the petitioner temporary exclusive use and occupancy of the dwelling that the parties share (Section 741.30(5)(a)(2), Florida Statutes) as well as temporary custody of a minor child or children of the parties (Section 741.30(5)(a)(3), Florida Statutes) ex parte, child support cannot be awarded until the final hearing. Find out whether paternity has ever been established and if the petitioner is already receiving child support in another case. Also ask whether the mother was legally married to a man who is not the other party in the current case when the child(ren) was conceived or born. If paternity has never been established, inform the petitioner that initiating a paternity case is one way to have child support established on a permanent basis.

Question 4

Does the judge have any options other than granting or denying the petition?
You answered Yes. This is correct. The court has three options when considering a petition:
  • Issue the temporary injunction which includes setting the matter for a final hearing;
  • Deny the temporary injunction and provide written explanation for doing so;
  • Enter an Order Setting Hearing Only.
While the Judge may not have found that the petition stated sufficient grounds for the issuance of a temporary injunction, the Judge may still want to speak to the parties in person in order to better understand the allegations in the petition.
